American Badger

The only badger native to North America—this solitary digger needs our help to survive.

Vital Signs

  • Common name: American Badger
  • Latin name: Taxidea taxus
  • Conservation status: Endangered
  • Range: British Columbia, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Alberta and Ontario
  • Lifespan: 4–5 years on average
  • Size: 50–80 cm long; 6–12 kg (males are larger)

American Badger Facts

  • Member of the mustelid family, related to weasels and wolverines.
  • Feeds on small rodents, birds, eggs, reptiles, amphibians and large invertebrates.
  • A solitary animal that’s active at night and in the early morning.
  • Digs extensive tunnel networks for shelter and rearing its young.
  • Breeds in summer; females delay implantation until February and give birth in early spring.
  • Not a true hibernator—badgers slow their metabolism during the coldest months to conserve energy.

Threats

American Badgers require open grasslands free of dense forest. Across Canada, agriculture, roads and urban development are fragmenting and destroying their habitat. Vehicle collisions are a major cause of mortality, and loss of grassland also reduces their prey.

What’s Being Done

Conservation teams are identifying badger habitats and threats across Canada. Subspecies of badger are listed under the federal Species at Risk Act and provincial legislation, which affords them legal protection on federal and provincial lands. Habitat protection programs around den sites help female badgers raise their young.

How You Can Help

  • Report sightings of badgers to provincial wildlife authorities and citizen science databases.
  • Report illegal trapping or harassment of badgers if you see it on protected lands.
  • If you have badger dens on your property, enquire about stewardship programs to safeguard them.
  • Stay informed about endangered species by subscribing to Nature Canada’s monthly e‑newsletter.
  • Add your voice to campaigns advocating for grassland protection and responsible land use.
